# Background

## Active Recall Testing

_Active recall testing_ means being asked a question and trying to remember the answer. This contrasts with _passive study_, where we consume information without pausing to test our knowledge. Research demonstrates that active recall testing is significantly more effective at building strong memories than passive study for two reasons:

- The act of recalling something _strengthens_ the memory, increasing the chances we'll be able to remember it again.
- When we're unable to answer a question, it tells us we need to return to the material to review or relearn it.

Good teachers integrate active recall testing through questions after readings and weekly tests. A practical approach involves _flashcards_ -- traditional paper cards with questions on one side and answers on the other. By not reviewing answers until after attempting recall, you learn more effectively than passive observation allows.

## Use It or Lose It

Our brains are efficient machines that rapidly forget information deemed not useful. Studies show we forget approximately 75% of material learned within a 48 hour period. However, the solution is straightforward: _review_. By reviewing newly-learned information, we can greatly reduce forgetting.

Traditionally, reviewing has been impractical with paper flashcards. While managing 30 cards is feasible, reviewing 300 or 3000 becomes unmanageable.

## Spaced Repetition

The _spacing effect_ was reported in 1885 by Hermann Ebbinghaus, a German psychologist who observed that spreading reviews over time produces more effective learning than multiple sessions in one day. This principle has evolved into what is called _spaced repetition_.

In 1972, Sebastian Leitner popularized a spaced repetition method using paper flashcards separated into boxes. Cards moved to different boxes based on successful or unsuccessful reviews, providing a rough estimate of knowledge and review timing. This system improved upon single-box approaches but offered rough estimates rather than exact dates.

SuperMemo, a commercial flashcard program, pioneered systems that track ideal review timing and optimize based on user performance. In SuperMemo's system, you provide feedback on each answer -- whether you forgot completely, made mistakes, or remembered easily -- and the program calculates optimal review timing. With each successful recall, the memory strengthens, extending the time between reviews. Material might be reviewed today, then 3 days later, 15 days later, 45 days later, and so on.

This approach enabled learning and retention with the absolute minimum amount of effort necessary.

## Why Anki?

While SuperMemo profoundly impacted the field, it has significant limitations: buggy operation, difficult navigation, Windows-only availability, proprietary restrictions on extension and data access, and limited free versions.

Anki addresses these issues. It offers free clients available on many platforms, making it accessible to students and teachers with budget constraints. As open source software, Anki features an already flourishing library of add-ons contributed by end-users. It runs on Windows, macOS, Linux/FreeBSD, and mobile devices, and it is considerably easier to use than SuperMemo.

Anki's spaced repetition system is based on the SM-2 algorithm. Recently, a new algorithm called FSRS has been integrated as an alternative to the legacy SM-2 algorithm.
